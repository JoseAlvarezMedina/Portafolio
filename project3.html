Crime in Context Analysis

Statistical analysis of violent crime in the U.S. (1975–2015)

What is Crime in Context?

Crime in Context is an in-depth study of the four major violent crimes (homicide, rape, assault and robbery) across 68 U.S. jurisdictions from 1975 to 2015. This project combines data cleaning, descriptive analysis and multivariate statistical models to reveal geographic and temporal patterns.

  • Data Loading & Preprocessing: Filtering records, handling missing values and outliers via IQR.
  • Exploratory Data Analysis: Boxplots, correlation matrices and visualizations with ggplot2 and factoextra.
  • PCA & LDA: Dimension reduction and classification by region and decade using prcomp() and MASS::lda().
  • Hierarchical Clustering: Grouping jurisdictions with hclust() and cutree().

Results highlight significant regional and temporal differences (MANOVA), though internal heterogeneity limits classification accuracy (~47%). Clustering suggests two main crime-behavior patterns.

Preprocessing

Normalization, duplicate removal and outlier handling to ensure data quality.

Exploratory Analysis

Visual exploration with boxplots and correlation matrices to uncover patterns.

Multivariate Modeling

PCA, LDA and MANOVA for dimension reduction and hypothesis testing.

Process Details

  1. Data Loading: Imported the “Crime in Context 1975–2015” dataset from Kaggle into R.
  2. Cleaning & Filtering: Removed incomplete records, duplicates and IQR-based outliers.
  3. EDA: Generated boxplots by region and crime-type correlation matrices.
  4. PCA: Identified principal components; assault & robbery explained most variance.
  5. LDA: Discriminated by region & decade (accuracy ≈ 47%).
  6. MANOVA: Conducted multivariate mean tests, confirming significant differences.
  7. Clustering: Performed hierarchical clustering, grouping jurisdictions into two main patterns.

Conclusions

While there are clear regional and temporal differences, internal variation within jurisdictions limits classification accuracy. Incorporating socio-economic variables could improve predictive power and yield deeper insights into crime drivers.
